## Why Gross Rental Yield Is a Misleading Metric

Most investors evaluate properties using gross rental yield — annual rent divided by property price. A property yielding "3.5%" sounds decent. But gross yield ignores maintenance, vacancy, property tax, repairs, insurance, and the cost of capital. The actual return is often 40-50% lower than the headline number.

A complete ROI evaluation requires five metrics, not one.

## The Five Metrics Framework

### 1. Net Rental Yield (Annual)
**Formula:** (Annual Rent - Annual Expenses) / Total Investment Cost

Deductions from gross rent:
- Maintenance charges: ₹3-12/sqft/month
- Property tax: 0.5-1.5% of property value annually
- Vacancy allowance: 5-10% of annual rent
- Repairs and upkeep: 0.5-1% of property value annually
- Insurance (optional): ₹2,000-5,000 annually

**Typical impact:** Net yield is 60-70% of gross yield. A 3.5% gross yield becomes 2.1-2.5% net.

### 2. Capital Appreciation (CAGR)
**Formula:** (Current Value / Purchase Price)^(1/years) - 1

India-wide averages by market type:
- Premium metro (Mumbai, Gurgaon premium): 4-8% CAGR
- Growth corridors (Sarjapur, Panvel, Hinjewadi): 12-18% CAGR
- Tier-2 cities: 8-14% CAGR
- Stagnant markets: 0-4% CAGR

### 3. Total Return (Yield + Appreciation)
**Formula:** Net Yield + Capital Appreciation CAGR

| Location Type | Net Yield | Appreciation | Total Return |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Electronic City, Bangalore | 2.8% | 10% | 12.8% |
| Sarjapur Road, Bangalore | 2.2% | 16% | 18.2% |
| Panvel, Mumbai | 2.0% | 15% | 17.0% |
| Wagholi, Pune | 3.0% | 11% | 14.0% |
| Andheri, Mumbai | 1.5% | 6% | 7.5% |

### 4. Cash-on-Cash Return (Leveraged)
**Formula:** Annual Cash Flow / Total Cash Invested

This measures return on YOUR money, not total property cost. With 80% leverage:
- Cash invested: 20% down payment + stamp duty + registration = ~28-30% of property value
- Annual cash flow: Rent - EMI - Expenses

| Leverage | Cash Invested (₹50L property) | Annual Cash Flow | Cash-on-Cash |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| No loan | ₹55L (all-in) | ₹1.2L | 2.2% |
| 60% LTV | ₹25L | -₹0.5L (negative) | Negative |
| 80% LTV | ₹15L | -₹1.5L (negative) | Negative |

**Key insight:** Leveraged residential property almost always has negative cash flow. The return comes from appreciation, not rental income.

### 5. Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
**Formula:** Discount rate that makes NPV of all cash flows = 0

IRR captures everything: purchase cost, rental income, expenses, EMIs, and final sale value. It's the single most comprehensive metric.

**Typical IRR for well-chosen Indian real estate:** 14-20% over 5-year holds in growth corridors.

## Common Evaluation Mistakes

1. **Ignoring transaction costs:** Stamp duty (5-7%), registration (1%), brokerage (1-2%), and GST (1-5%) add 8-15% to acquisition cost
2. **Projecting current rents forward:** Rents grow at 5-8% annually in strong markets — factor this into IRR calculations
3. **Ignoring exit costs:** Selling incurs capital gains tax (20% LTCG with indexation), brokerage (1-2%), and time-to-sell (2-6 months)
4. **Comparing gross yields across cities:** A 3% yield in Mumbai (₹1.5Cr property) is not equivalent to 3% in Pune (₹50L property) — risk profiles differ

## The Bottom Line

Gross rental yield tells you almost nothing about actual returns. Use the five-metric framework: net yield for income assessment, CAGR for growth, total return for the complete picture, cash-on-cash for leverage impact, and IRR for the definitive single number.
