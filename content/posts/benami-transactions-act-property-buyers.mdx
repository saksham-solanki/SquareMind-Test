## What Is a Benami Transaction?

A benami transaction is where a property is purchased by one person but held in the name of another person ("benamidar"). The real owner (beneficial owner) pays for the property but keeps it in someone else's name — typically to evade taxes, hide assets, or circumvent legal restrictions.

## The Prohibition of Benami Property Transactions Act, 2016

The updated Benami Act (2016 amendment to the 1988 Act) significantly strengthened enforcement:

| Provision | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Benami property | Can be confiscated by government |
| Penalty (beneficial owner) | Up to 25% of property value |
| Prison term | Up to 7 years |
| Benamidar (person in whose name) | Also liable for penalty |
| Limitation period | No time limit for detection |

## What Qualifies as Benami?

### Benami (Illegal)
- Father buys property in son's name using undisclosed income
- Business owner registers property in employee's name
- Politician holds property through shell companies

### Not Benami (Legal)
- Property purchased in spouse's name from disclosed income (joint family arrangement)
- Property in HUF member's name using HUF funds
- Property held by a trustee for a documented trust
- Property purchased by one person in another's name with proper documentation and disclosed source

## How It Affects Legitimate Buyers

### Risk: Buying Property from a Benami Owner

If you unknowingly purchase property that is later identified as benami:
- The government can confiscate the property
- You may lose your investment
- Recovery from the seller may be difficult

### Protection Measures

1. Verify the seller's source of funds for original purchase
2. Check if the registered owner and actual occupant are the same
3. Look for signs of proxy ownership (someone else managing the property)
4. Verify property tax is paid by the registered owner
5. Check if the property has been under Benami investigation (IT department)

## Due Diligence Steps

1. **Title search:** Verify all previous ownership transfers have legitimate consideration
2. **Tax compliance:** Check if seller has declared the property in their income tax returns
3. **Occupation history:** Verify who has been using/occupying the property
4. **Payment trail:** Ensure all payments go to the registered owner's bank account
